Spatial Confounding · NYC Health Data

ACS 2019 5-yr · OLS · CAR / BYM2
Every map of New York City tells a story — but not always the story we think it does. The uninsured rate across NYC census tracts (2019 ACS 5-year estimates) shows a striking geographic pattern. The Bronx and parts of Queens and Brooklyn stand out immediately.

Before drawing conclusions about why, there is a question worth pausing on: Is this pattern revealing something about healthcare access — or about deeper structural forces that shaped these neighborhoods over decades?
The geographic forces that concentrated poverty and unemployment in certain neighborhoods also concentrated uninsurance. When predictors and outcomes share the same spatial pattern, standard regression can mistake one for the other. That is spatial confounding — and it is far more common in health and social data than most analyses acknowledge.

A natural first step is to ask: does poverty (or unemployment) predict the uninsured rate? The scatterplot shows a strong, significant relationship. The coefficient looks precise, the R² is respectable. It looks like a clean answer.

But precision is not the same as accuracy. Standard regression treats each tract as an independent observation. In reality, neighboring tracts are not independent — they share decades of history, infrastructure, and policy. When we map the model's residuals (the errors left behind after fitting), they cluster geographically. Neighboring tracts have similar residuals — a clear violation of the independence assumption.

This means the model silently absorbed spatial structure from the data and attributed part of it to the predictor. The coefficient is inflated.
A significant p-value does not rule out spatial confounding. Clustered residuals on a map are the diagnostic signal — they tell you the model's independence assumption is violated and that the coefficient estimate may be biased. Moran's I on the residuals should be near zero if the model were correctly specified.

The BYM2 model (Besag-York-Mollié) is designed for exactly this situation. It separates the predictor's independent effect from the underlying spatial structure of neighborhoods. It asks: after accounting for the tendency of nearby tracts to be similar, how much of the relationship between our predictor and uninsurance actually remains?

The spatial random effect captures what belongs to place — the neighborhood geography that the naive model was quietly incorporating into the predictor's coefficient. Once that spatial structure is modeled explicitly, the coefficient for the predictor changes. Often it shrinks. This is not a problem with the data or the predictor. It is a more honest answer.
A smaller BYM2 coefficient does not mean the relationship is unimportant. It means we can now distinguish how much of the disparity is driven by the predictor versus how much belongs to the geography of place itself — and that distinction is exactly what determines where and how to intervene.

Methods note: The BYM2 spatial random effect uses a CAR (conditional autoregressive) approximation via iterative spatial smoothing of the residuals from the OLS fit. The mixing parameter φ represents the fraction of random-effect variance attributable to the structured (spatially correlated) component vs. unstructured noise.
