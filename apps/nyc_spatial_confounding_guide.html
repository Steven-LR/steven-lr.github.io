Spatial Confounding · NYC Health Data

OLS · CAR · BYM2
Every map of New York City tells a story — but not always the story we think it does. Below is the uninsured rate across NYC census tracts from the 2019 ACS. The pattern is striking: the Bronx and parts of Queens and Brooklyn stand out immediately.

Before drawing any conclusions about why — or what to do about it — there is a question worth pausing on: Is this pattern telling us about healthcare access, or is it telling us about something deeper in how neighborhoods were shaped over decades?
The same geographic forces that concentrated poverty and unemployment in certain neighborhoods also concentrated uninsurance. When variables share the same spatial pattern, standard analysis can mistake one for the other. That is spatial confounding.

A common first step in health data analysis is to ask: does poverty (or unemployment) predict the uninsured rate? The scatterplot below runs that regression — and it finds a strong, significant relationship. It looks like a clean answer.

But there is a hidden problem. Standard regression assumes each tract is an independent data point. In reality, neighboring tracts are not independent — they share history, infrastructure, and policy. When we map the residuals (the "leftovers" after the model explains what it can), they cluster spatially. That means the model quietly borrowed geographic signal and attributed it to the predictor. The coefficient is inflated.
A statistically significant result is not the same as a causally clean result. When residuals cluster on a map, that is the data telling you spatial confounding is present.

The BYM2 model (Besag-York-Mollié) is designed for exactly this situation. It separates the predictor's effect from the underlying spatial structure of neighborhoods. It asks: after accounting for the fact that nearby tracts tend to be similar, how much of the relationship between poverty (or unemployment) and uninsurance is still there?

The answer is almost always: less than the naive model suggested. Sometimes the coefficient shrinks substantially. Sometimes it changes direction. This is not a failure — it is a more honest picture. The spatial random effect absorbs the neighborhood-level geography that was confounding the estimate.
A smaller coefficient after spatial adjustment does not mean the relationship disappeared. It means we now know how much of the association was driven by place itself — and that is exactly the kind of insight that should inform where and how you intervene.

Methods note: The BYM2 spatial random effect shown here uses a CAR (conditional autoregressive) approximation via eigenvector decomposition of the queen-contiguity spatial weights matrix, computed from tract centroids. The mixing parameter φ is estimated as the proportion of total random-effect variance attributable to the structured spatial component. This is a pedagogical approximation; production analyses should use INLA or Stan with full Bayesian inference.
