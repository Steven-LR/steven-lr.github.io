BYM2 · Model Reference

What is BYM2?

The BYM2 model (Riebler et al., 2016) is a reparameterisation of the classical Besag-York-Mollié (BYM) model for areal spatial data. It is widely used in disease mapping and spatial epidemiology to decompose regional variation into a structured spatial component and an unstructured iid component.

The central innovation is a single mixing parameter φ ∈ [0, 1] that controls how much of the total variance is attributable to spatial autocorrelation vs. pure noise — and this parameter is interpretable on a common scale regardless of the graph structure.

The Outcome Model

Counts yᵢ for area i follow a Poisson distribution:

yᵢ | λᵢ ~ Poisson( Eᵢ · λᵢ ) log λᵢ = β₀ + bᵢ

where Eᵢ is the expected count (population-at-risk × reference rate), β₀ is the log baseline rate, and bᵢ is the BYM2 random effect.

The BYM2 Random Effect

bᵢ = σ · ( √(1 − φ) · εᵢ + √φ · uᵢ ) εᵢ ~ iid N(0, 1) (unstructured noise) u ~ N(0, Q★⁻¹) (spatially structured ICAR)

The vector u is drawn from an intrinsic CAR (ICAR) distribution whose precision matrix Q★ is scaled so that its average marginal variance equals 1 — making σ² the true marginal variance of bᵢ regardless of φ.

Key insight: In the classical BYM model, the two variance parameters were not separately identifiable and their scales depended on the graph. BYM2 fixes this by scaling Q★ so that σ and φ are always on a consistent, interpretable scale.

Grid & Outcome Parameters

SymbolDescription
n Grid dimension. The spatial domain is an n × n lattice of equally-sized areas, giving N = n² total regions.
β₀ Log baseline rate. The expected SMR when all random effects are zero is exp(β₀). Set β₀ = 0 for an expected SMR of 1.0 (null baseline).
E Expected count per area. Larger E means more observed counts, which reduces Poisson noise and lets the spatial signal in bᵢ dominate. Think of E as the population size — larger populations give cleaner spatial patterns.

BYM2 Parameters

SymbolDescription
φ (phi) Spatial mixing proportion ∈ [0, 1]. Controls the fraction of total variance attributable to the structured ICAR component.

φ = 0 → purely iid noise, no spatial autocorrelation.
φ = 1 → purely spatial ICAR, maximum autocorrelation.
φ = 0.5 → equal mix of structured and unstructured.
σ (sigma) Marginal standard deviation of the random effects bᵢ. Controls overall heterogeneity across areas. Because Q★ is scaled to have average marginal variance = 1, σ directly sets the spread of bᵢ regardless of φ or the graph structure.

Weight Matrix

TypeDescription
Queen (8-nbr) Cell (r, c) is a neighbour of all 8 surrounding cells — horizontally, vertically, and diagonally adjacent. Produces more neighbours per cell, a denser precision matrix, and smoother spatial fields.
Rook (4-nbr) Cell (r, c) is a neighbour only of its 4 cardinal neighbours (up, down, left, right). Sparser graph. Interior cells have exactly 4 neighbours; edge and corner cells fewer.
Effect on Moran's I: Switching from Rook to Queen adds diagonal neighbours, increasing S₀ (sum of weights). For the same spatial field, this generally produces a slightly lower raw Moran's I statistic but does not change the underlying spatial pattern — only the definition of "neighbour."

Step-by-Step Data Generation

  1. Build the adjacency matrix W.
    For an n × n grid, W is an N × N binary matrix where Wᵢⱼ = 1 if areas i and j are neighbours (Queen or Rook), 0 otherwise. W is symmetric with zero diagonal.
  2. Construct the ICAR precision matrix Q.
    Q = D − W where D = diag(rowSums(W)) is the degree matrix. Q is the graph Laplacian — it is positive semi-definite with rank N − 1 (one zero eigenvalue corresponding to the constant vector, reflecting the sum-to-zero constraint of the ICAR).
  3. Scale Q to Q★ (the BYM2 scaling).
    Eigen-decompose Q = VΛVᵀ. The generalised inverse is Q⁻ = V₊Λ₊⁻¹V₊ᵀ where + denotes retaining only eigenvectors with λ > tol. Compute the geometric mean of the marginal variances:
    s = exp( mean( log( diag(Q⁻) ) ) )
    Then Q★ = Q / (1/s) = s · Q, so that mean(diag(Q★⁻)) = 1. The pseudo-inverse Q★⁻¹ is pre-computed for sampling.
  4. Sample the ICAR component u.
    Draw u ~ N(0, Q★⁻¹) using the eigendecomposition: u = V₊ Λ₊^{-½} z where z ~ N(0, I). This samples from the proper approximation of the intrinsic CAR.
  5. Sample the iid component ε.
    Draw ε ~ N(0, I_N) — N independent standard normals.
  6. Mix into BYM2 random effects b.
    bᵢ = σ · ( √(1−φ) · εᵢ + √φ · uᵢ )
    Both components have marginal variance 1 (by construction of Q★ and ε), so Var(bᵢ) = σ² for any φ.
  7. Generate Poisson counts.
    For each area i, draw:
    yᵢ ~ Poisson( E · exp(β₀ + bᵢ) )
    The SMR (Standardised Mortality/Morbidity Ratio) is SMRᵢ = yᵢ / E. Moran's I is computed on log(SMRᵢ + 0.01) to stabilise variance.
Why does Poisson noise obscure the spatial signal? The BYM2 field bᵢ encodes a smooth spatial pattern, but yᵢ is a random draw. When E is small, Poisson variability (≈ √E / E = 1/√E relative SD) can be large relative to σ. The SMR is then dominated by sampling noise rather than the underlying spatial field. Increasing E or σ lets the spatial signal "win."

Moran's I Statistic

Moran's I measures global spatial autocorrelation — whether areas with similar values tend to cluster together. It is computed here on the row-standardised weight matrix.

N ΣᵢΣⱼ wᵢⱼ (zᵢ − z̄)(zⱼ − z̄) I = ─── · ──────────────────────────────── S₀ Σᵢ (zᵢ − z̄)² where zᵢ = log(SMRᵢ + 0.01), S₀ = Σᵢ Σⱼ wᵢⱼ

Null Expectation

Under the null hypothesis of no spatial autocorrelation (random permutation), the expected value of I is:

E[I] = −1 / (N − 1)

For large N this approaches 0, but for small grids it is noticeably negative (e.g., for N = 25: E[I] = −0.042).

Inference

The p-value is computed under a normal approximation using the analytical variance of I under the normality assumption (using S₀, S₁, S₂ — the sums of weights, squared weights, and squared row+column sums respectively). A two-sided test is used:

Z = (I − E[I]) / √Var(I) p-value = 2 · (1 − Φ(|Z|))

Interpretation

I valueInterpretation
I ≫ E[I], p < 0.05Positive spatial autocorrelation — similar values cluster together. High-value areas neighbour high-value areas.
I ≈ E[I]No evidence of spatial autocorrelation. Values appear randomly distributed across the grid.
I ≪ E[I], p < 0.05Negative spatial autocorrelation (dispersion) — high-value areas tend to be surrounded by low-value areas, creating a checkerboard pattern.

Building Intuition

φ → 1: The random effect is purely ICAR. You'll see smooth, large-scale spatial gradients on the map and Moran's I will be high. This is the regime where spatial clustering is "baked in."
φ → 0: Pure iid noise. The map looks like TV static. Moran's I should be near E[I] = −1/(N−1) and the p-value non-significant. Any clustering you see is pure chance.
Large E (e.g. 500): Poisson sampling variance is small relative to the spatial signal. The SMR map closely mirrors the latent bᵢ field. Moran's I will reliably reflect φ.
Small E (e.g. 10): Poisson noise dominates. Even with high φ, the SMR surface looks noisy and Moran's I may not be significant. This is why "Hunt for significance" sometimes needs multiple retries — the same underlying spatial field generates different observed count surfaces each time.

Why Does the Hunt Cap at 10 Tries?

When the Poisson signal-to-noise ratio is very low (small E, small σ, or low φ), each realisation of the count process may independently fail to achieve p < 0.05 even though the true field has spatial structure. After 10 tries, the last realisation is shown so you can see what the model is producing under these difficult conditions — rather than looping indefinitely.
